## 2.1 Matrices as transformations

### A matrix is a function

Beats:

- $A$ applied to $\mathbf{x}$ gives a new vector; read it as a function $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$. Introduce **transformation**.
- An $m \times n$ matrix maps $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$; when $m = n$ the space maps to itself (where rotation, scaling, projection live).
- The stance shift: stop asking what the numbers in $A$ *are*, ask what $A$ *does*.

$$T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}, \qquad A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n},\quad \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n},\quad A\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}.$$

### The simplest transformation is a difference

Beats:

- The **difference matrix** as the first concrete transformation: it replaces each entry with its increment from the previous one (cumulative measurements in, increments out).
- The point is the stance, not the arithmetic. *(Source: course L2.)*

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 - x_1 \\ x_3 - x_2 \end{bmatrix}.$$

### Linearity

Beats:

- Define **linear**: respects addition and scaling (the identity below).
- Every matrix is a linear map; every finite-dimensional linear map is some matrix.
- Geometric face: origin to origin, lines to lines, even spacing preserved; can rotate/stretch/flatten/shear, cannot bend.
- Why it matters for estimation: a transformation's effect on a whole dataset is fixed by its effect on a few directions.

$$T(a\mathbf{x} + b\mathbf{y}) = a\,T(\mathbf{x}) + b\,T(\mathbf{y}).$$

### The column picture

Beats (this is the chapter's spine, give it room):

- Read $A\mathbf{x}$ as a **linear combination of the columns** of $A$, weights = entries of $\mathbf{x}$. Name it the **column picture**.
- Reframe: $\mathbf{x}$ is a recipe (how much of each column), not data being acted on; reachable outputs = what the columns can build.
- Forward hooks: solvability (2.4), projection onto the column span (Ch 11), the directions a dataset occupies (Ch 10).
- Worked read of the difference matrix both ways: columns $(1,-1,0), (0,1,-1), (0,0,1)$; row-by-row it is differences, column-by-column it is a recipe over three fixed directions.

$$A\mathbf{x} = x_1 \mathbf{a}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{a}_n.$$
