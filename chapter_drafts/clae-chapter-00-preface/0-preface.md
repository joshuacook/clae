# Preface

> **BEAT (cold open — the room):** 2012. A night class that ended at eight. First quiz back: **5 out of 25**. Verbatim: "I sat in the room until 10 o'clock trying to figure out the answer to everything I've gotten wrong." The deciding was the staying — he never decided to stay in the degree; he stayed in the room. The reader who is twelve years rusty has their own 5/25 somewhere.

> **BEAT (who I was):** Before that room: taught rigorous proof-based geometry, in Spanish, to students the system had left behind — proofs as "a way to bridge some of that loss they had experienced by having bad teachers." Lockhart's Lament as the standing complaint: "we make students copy sheet music for 10 years before we play them a song." He taught against it — then went back to school at 37 for a second bachelor's in applied math. Verbatim, of the linalg class: "the most difficult class I've ever taken … I thought this is the end for me, this quixotic quest ends here."

> **BEAT (the teacher):** Verbatim: "**Jim Kruidenier at Santa Barbara City College is a mystic steeped in the ways of strang.**" Strang by transmission — Kruidenier had read and watched (MIT OCW) Strang; Josh never met Strang in person. The re-enchantment was passed person-to-person. Stance, verbatim: "It was no secret" — Kruidenier taught the connections in the open. This book's narrator is an initiate showing you openly, not a magician hoarding; the book is the Kruidenier for readers who never got one.

> **BEAT (the click):** Verbatim: "basis was the jewel." Waves physics ran concurrently — linear combinations in two rooms the same semester; superposition as axpy, Fourier coefficients as weights — and the same word, *basis*, named the sinusoids under a Fourier series and the vectors under a coordinate list. The cross-domain recognition happened in the moment, not in hindsight. (Optional texture: Kruidenier's trig magic from calc 2 — the whole identity sheet from a+b and a−b, Pythagoras a corollary — see chapter_notes; placement may be here or a Ch 2 footnote.)

> **BEAT (the conversion image):** Strang's Chapter 4 — the projection picture, y hanging off the column space, the perpendicular dropped. Verbatim: "4 is WHERE IT CLICKED where I cracked my knuckles and said **« I see you Gil »**." This book's Part III climax (Ch 11) is literally the drawing that converted its author; the preface and Chapter 11 hold hands across the book.

> **BEAT (the payoff):** Second year: vector calc, then ODEs with the same teacher — "discovering linalg again"; Stephanie Frank Singer's *Linearity, Symmetry, and Prediction in the Hydrogen Atom* read off-syllabus in that era. Then CSUN: "pdes was almost … easy." The subject became one "where I would sit with friends over a beer and discuss it," and the foundation of a second professional career.

> **BEAT (the promise):** The audience named plainly: you took linear algebra, maybe more than once, and either never got excited or lost the excitement. Mission, verbatim: "how do I show that to someone who never had that, or remind someone that did have that, that it's missing from their life." The register the book will reach for, verbatim: "the principles of linearity are the coolest things ever — like, since Euclid, since the five postulates. These are fundamental truths of the nature of existence." The promise: re-enchantment, not review. This book plays the song.
