# Preface

The first quiz came back with a five on it. Five out of twenty-five, in a linear algebra night class at Santa Barbara City College, 2013. The class let out at eight. I sat in that room until ten o'clock trying to figure out the answer to everything I had gotten wrong.

I was thirty-seven years old. I had gone back to school for a second bachelor's degree, this one in applied mathematics, and that quiz was the first hard evidence that I had made a terrible mistake. I remember the thought precisely: this is the end for me. This quixotic quest ends here.

Some context for how strange that moment was. I was not new to mathematics and I was not new to classrooms. I had taught high school geometry, real geometry, two-column proofs and rigor, in Spanish, in schools where most of my students had been failed for years by the system they were sitting in. Paul Lockhart wrote that we teach mathematics the way we would teach music if we made students copy sheet music for ten years before ever playing them a song. I had built a teaching practice against that; proof was how I tried to give my students back something school had taken from them. And then I sat down in a community college linear algebra class and drowned.

The teacher was Jim Kruidenier, and I want his name in this book. Jim is a mystic steeped in the ways of Gilbert Strang. He had read Strang, watched the MIT OpenCourseWare lectures, absorbed the whole constructive way of seeing the subject, and he taught it in the open. No secrets, nothing held back for the initiated. I never met Strang. I met Jim, and that turned out to be how this subject actually travels: person to person, one teacher who caught it showing the next where to look.

What flipped it for me was a word. That semester I was also taking waves in physics, and somewhere in the middle of both courses the same word surfaced in each: basis. The sinusoids under a Fourier series and the vectors under a coordinate list, one word for both, and both courses meaning precisely the same thing by it. Basis was the jewel. A list of numbers was never the vector; it was a recipe written against a chosen basis, and superposition was the same recipe running in the other room. I saw the connection then, in the moment, and I can still feel it.

Then came chapter 4. In Strang's book that is orthogonality: the picture of a vector hanging just off a subspace, a perpendicular dropped to the nearest point, and the discovery that the entire machinery of regression falls out of that one drawing. I cracked my knuckles and said, out loud, to a textbook: I see you, Gil. If this book has a single destination, it is that drawing. You will meet it, properly earned, in Chapter 11.

The rest of the story is the part I wish for you. The next year Jim marched us through vector calculus and differential equations, and I got to discover linear algebra a second time as it surfaced inside everything else. Somewhere in that stretch I found Stephanie Frank Singer's book on the hydrogen atom, off syllabus, and learned that electron orbitals are a basis, which still strikes me as a fact the universe did not have to grant us. By the time I transferred to Cal State Northridge, partial differential equations felt almost easy. The class that nearly ended me became the foundation of my second career, and the subject became one I would sit and argue about with friends over a beer.

So here is who I think you are. You took linear algebra, maybe more than once. You know the words: span, basis, eigenvalue, least squares. You may use some of them at work, at arm's length, through a library call. And somewhere between the axioms and the exams, nobody played you the song. Or someone did, years ago, and the melody has gone missing.

I believe the principles of linearity are among the coolest things our species has found, in the lineage of Euclid's five postulates: a few plain assumptions that turn out to hold up an enormous share of what we can compute about the world. This book asks for two operations and closure, and spends fourteen chapters showing you what that contract buys. Everything in it runs: every figure and every number on these pages is produced by code you can execute yourself. And all of it aims at the question that hooked me in that night class. Of all the linear combinations available, which one is the estimate?

You have your own five out of twenty-five somewhere. Stay in the room.
